Best PC & Steam Deck Settings for LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight (July 2026)

Published: July 10, 2026 · 7 min read

Seven weeks after launch, tech outlets have finally put real benchmarking hours into LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight, and the consensus is clear: the game's Epic preset is not the way to play it. Between a detailed PC tuning breakdown from Wccftech, a lighter community preset from Destructoid, and ongoing Steam Deck testing from Steam Deck HQ, there's now enough real-world data to build a proper settings guide instead of just guessing. Here's what actually moves the needle, on both PC and Steam Deck.

The Core Problem: Shader Compilation Stutter

Before touching any sliders, it's worth understanding why the game stutters in the first place. According to Wccftech's tuning guide, Legacy of the Dark Knight's Unreal Engine 5 pipeline state object (PSO) compilation step is incomplete and misses some crucial PSOs, which causes shader compilation stutter during normal gameplay, plus separate, more jarring hitches when transitioning between cutscenes and gameplay. No graphics setting fully eliminates this — it's a code-level issue TT Games would need to patch — but the settings below reduce how often you'll notice it.

Wccftech also flagged that the official minimum-spec sheet targets 30 FPS at 1080p only with Frame Generation enabled, which effectively implies a native framerate closer to 15 FPS before interpolation — a target the outlet called "not acceptable" as a baseline. The good news, confirmed by both Wccftech's testing and Steam Deck HQ's hands-on impressions, is that real-world performance is noticeably better than that spec sheet suggests once you actually tune the settings.

Optimized PC Settings: +44% Average FPS

Wccftech benchmarked the game on an RTX 4090 / Core i7-14700K system at 1440p, comparing the maxed-out Epic preset against a custom-tuned profile. The result was a 44% increase in average framerate, with 1% and 0.1% lows (the stutter-sensitive metrics) improving by 38% each:

SettingEpic PresetOptimized
Average FPS106153 (+44%)
1% Low FPS79109 (+38%)
0.1% Low FPS6995 (+38%)

The optimized profile keeps Lighting, Shadows, Material Quality, Effects, and Reflections at High rather than Epic, drops View Distance to Medium, and leaves Streaming Distance and Population Quality at High so Gotham doesn't feel empty. Two settings stood out as counter-intuitive: Anisotropic Filtering should stay at 16X, since Wccftech reproducibly measured worse performance at 4X or below, and LEGO Mesh Quality is best left at Epic, since lowering it barely helped framerate but visibly blocked out LEGO assets. Textures are a VRAM call rather than a pure performance one: Epic needs 12GB+ of VRAM at 1440p, so 8GB cards should drop to High or Medium and lean on upscaling instead.

Upscaler pick: use whichever matches your GPU vendor — DLSS Super Resolution for NVIDIA RTX, FSR for AMD Radeon, XeSS for Intel Arc — and fall back to the engine's native TSR only if none of those are available.

A Lighter Community Preset for Mid-Range Hardware

Not everyone is running a 4090. Destructoid's Arka Sarkar, testing on a Ryzen 7 5700X with 16GB of RAM and a mid-range Radeon RX 9060 XT, published a simpler preset built around a hard 60 FPS frame cap rather than leaving the limiter unlocked, which they found introduced occasional stutters. Their setup runs Fullscreen mode, VSync off, Anti-Aliasing and Upscaling off (since the card didn't need the extra help), Lighting Quality High, and most other sliders — Shadows, Textures, Material Quality, Reflections, and Population Quality — set to Medium. Anisotropic Filtering was set lower, to 8X, though this diverges from Wccftech's finding above; if you notice odd performance dips, try setting it back to 16X. If you're still seeing stutter on similar hardware, Destructoid's advice is to keep stepping individual Medium settings down to Low one at a time rather than dropping everything at once.

Steam Deck: Verified and Playable, But Lock Your Framerate

Legacy of the Dark Knight earned Valve's Steam Deck Verified badge on May 12, 2026, and Steam Deck HQ's hands-on testing backs that up with real numbers. The game defaults to Low settings with FSR upscaling set to Balanced and Frame Generation off — and according to Steam Deck HQ, Frame Generation isn't actually needed to keep the game playable, despite what the official spec sheet implies.

Performance splits cleanly into two zones. In contained story levels, the Deck holds 40–60 FPS, with occasional dips to 37–38 FPS during the busiest fights. Out in open-world Gotham, framerate generally sits at 34–37 FPS while on foot, but drops to roughly 30 FPS once you're driving the Batmobile, with more visible stutter while boosting. Steam Deck HQ's recommendation: lock the framerate to 30 FPS through the SteamOS frame limiter for the whole game rather than leaving it uncapped, since the jump between a smooth 60 FPS indoors and a stuttery uncapped open world feels far worse than a consistent 30. The game also supports the Deck's native 16:10 aspect ratio with no black bars, and HDR (on OLED models) with minimal performance cost.

Quick Settings Cheat Sheet

  • High-end PC (12GB+ VRAM): High preset with Anisotropic Filtering at 16X and LEGO Mesh Quality at Epic — up to 44% faster than maxed-out Epic
  • Mid-range PC: Mostly Medium settings, hard 60 FPS cap, native upscaler for your GPU vendor
  • Steam Deck: Keep the Low/FSR Balanced default, leave Frame Generation off, lock to 30 FPS via the SteamOS limiter
  • All platforms: Some shader stutter and cutscene-transition hitching is a known engine-level issue, not a settings problem

None of this fixes the underlying PSO compilation gap, so don't be surprised if a stutter slips through even on the optimized profile — that part is on TT Games to patch.
