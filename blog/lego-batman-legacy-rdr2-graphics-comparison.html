Why Fans Can't Stop Comparing LEGO Batman: Legacy to Red Dead Redemption 2

Published: June 9, 2026 · 6 min read

Three weeks after launch, LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight has spawned one of the more unexpected gaming debates of 2026: is TT Games' brick-built open world actually more visually impressive than Rockstar's legendary Red Dead Redemption 2? The comparison has spread far enough that even the official LEGO Group social media account has weighed in — with a note of weary amusement.

How the Trend Started

Shortly after Legacy of the Dark Knight's launch on May 22, 2026, social media began filling with side-by-side video comparisons. The subject: rainfall on concrete. It sounds mundane, but the way water pools, runs, and reflects ambient light in Legacy of the Dark Knight's Gotham City caught players off guard. Clips showing Gotham's rain-slicked streets next to similar scenes from Red Dead Redemption 2 — still widely considered the graphical benchmark for open-world games — began circulating widely, with viewers debating which looked more realistic.

The comparisons didn't stay limited to rain. Snow displacement, ambient occlusion around environmental objects, and the way artificial light from neon signs bleeds onto wet surfaces all drew similar scrutiny. According to reporting by Screen Rant (May 25, 2026), the trend became widespread enough that the LEGO Group's official X account felt compelled to acknowledge it — admitting the comparisons were becoming something of a running joke, while noting that the quality being highlighted was very much intentional.

"Surpassed by LEGO Batman."

— @poliwa_93 on X, captioning a side-by-side weather effects comparison, May 2026

What Unreal Engine 5 Actually Changes

The technology behind Legacy of the Dark Knight's visuals is Unreal Engine 5 — a first for the LEGO game franchise, which previously ran on TT Games' proprietary in-house engine. The switch represents the single largest technical leap in the series' history, and its effects are visible everywhere.

UE5's Lumen global illumination system is arguably the biggest contributor to the comparisons. Lumen calculates how light bounces between surfaces in real time, rather than relying on baked, pre-calculated lighting. In practice, this means a neon sign in Gotham doesn't just light up a fixed circle on the ground below it — it illuminates the underside of a passing vehicle, reflects in a puddle, and creates a subtle ambient glow that shifts as objects move through it. Red Dead Redemption 2, for all its visual achievements, uses a different generation of rendering technology, and the difference becomes apparent in exactly these kinds of dynamic lighting scenarios.

Nanite, UE5's virtualized geometry system, is similarly relevant. It allows the game to render extraordinarily detailed geometry — in this case, the surface detail of LEGO bricks themselves — without the kind of polygon budget compromises that would have been necessary on older engines. Players have noted that you can see the subtle imperfections on brick surfaces: mold lines, slight surface texture variation, even what appear to be fingerprint smudges on character minifigures in close-up cutscenes. This level of material detail on what are nominally plastic toys is a significant part of what's driving the comparisons.

Is the Comparison Actually Fair?

The short answer is: not entirely, and the gaming community knows it. As one commenter noted in the Screen Rant article's thread, "apart from both being open world, they share nothing in common" — and that's a fair point. Red Dead Redemption 2 launched in 2018 on hardware from that era; LEGO Batman: Legacy launched in 2026 on PlayStation 5, Xbox Series X|S, and high-end PCs with eight more years of hardware advancement baked in.

That said, the comparisons aren't entirely unfair either. RDR2 has benefited from years of PC mods and continues to receive updates, and it still holds up impressively by 2026 standards. The fact that a LEGO game — a franchise historically associated with accessible, family-friendly visuals rather than graphical showcase — is even in the conversation speaks to how dramatically TT Games has raised its technical ambitions.

OpenCritic data: Legacy of the Dark Knight currently holds a Top Critic Average of 84/100 with a 93% recommendation rate, per OpenCritic. Multiple review outlets specifically praised the game's visual presentation alongside its gameplay.

The "Arkham-ification" of the LEGO Aesthetic

Beyond raw technical specs, the comparisons point to something more fundamental about Legacy of the Dark Knight's artistic direction. Multiple critics and players have noted that the game draws clear visual inspiration from the Batman: Arkham series — darker atmospheric lighting, rain-soaked streets, and a Gotham City that feels genuinely oppressive rather than colorfully toy-like. This is, historically, unusual territory for a LEGO title.

The original LEGO Batman games used bright, flat lighting that made brick-built environments read clearly and cheerfully. Legacy of the Dark Knight retains the LEGO humor and minifigure aesthetic but wraps it in visual language borrowed from Rocksteady's Arkham trilogy. The result is a game that looks simultaneously like a LEGO set and a serious Batman story — and it's that unusual combination that seems to be fueling the viral comparisons. Nobody expects a LEGO game to look this atmospheric, which is precisely why screenshots and video comparisons generate so much reaction.

What TT Games Said

In a post-launch developer roundup covered by multiple outlets, TT Games developers noted that creating a version of Gotham that felt "real enough to be atmospheric, but stylized enough to be unmistakably LEGO" was one of the core visual challenges of the project. The team spent considerable time iterating on how surface materials — brick studs, transparent parts, rubber tires — should behave under different lighting conditions. The goal, according to developer comments, was that players should always feel like they could reach out and pick up the objects they were looking at.

Whether or not that goal has been fully achieved is a matter of some debate — the game's weather and lighting effects receive near-universal praise, while some players find that very detailed geometry on characters can occasionally produce an uncanny effect in longer cutscenes. But as a statement of technical intent, Legacy of the Dark Knight is clearly operating at a different level than its predecessors.

Worth exploring: If you want to see the visual effects that are driving these comparisons, the Gotham City open-world sections at night during rain — accessible from the early chapters — are typically where the most striking lighting work is visible. Head to the waterfront district for the densest concentration of neon reflections.

The Bigger Picture

The RDR2 comparison trend is, ultimately, a community in-joke that points toward something real: LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight has genuinely surprised players who came in with calibrated expectations for what a LEGO game looks like. The franchise has spent eighteen years building an association with accessible, cheerful visuals, and watching it suddenly produce screenshots that spark graphical benchmark debates is the kind of pleasant surprise that generates lasting online conversation.

Whether TT Games has "beaten" Red Dead Redemption 2 is the wrong question. What's clear is that the team has produced a game that forces a revision of what "LEGO graphics" means — and that, for a franchise turning 18 years old, is a significant achievement regardless of how you rank the rain effects.

As for the official LEGO Group X account's response to being pulled into the debate? According to Screen Rant's reporting, they acknowledged the comparison had gotten repetitive, but didn't exactly push back on the underlying premise. For a toy company whose products are getting compared to one of the most graphically celebrated games ever made, that seems like a reasonable reaction.
