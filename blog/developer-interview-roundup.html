How TT Games Built the Ultimate Batman Game: Developer Insights from Launch Week

📅 May 23, 2026  |  ⏱ 7 min read  |  By BrickHeroGuide Staff

With LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight now officially live — and already sitting at over 30,000 peak concurrent Steam players with an 84 on Metacritic — two members of the TT Games development team sat down with the press on launch day to pull back the curtain on how it all came together.

In separate interviews published May 22, strategic director Jonathan Smith spoke to Variety about the creative vision of merging every era of Batman, while design director Jimmy Sedota told GAMINGbible about the mechanical decisions behind character depth, the Arkham inspiration, and those Riddler puzzles. Together, the two interviews paint the clearest picture yet of what TT Games was trying to achieve — and why it landed so well.

"A Universal Solvent for the Imagination"

The central design question facing Legacy of the Dark Knight was one no Batman game had ever attempted: how do you bring together 80-plus years of Batman across dozens of wildly different interpretations — Adam West's campy 1966 hero, Tim Burton's gothic noir, Christopher Nolan's gritty realism, Matt Reeves's detective thriller — without the seams showing?

For Smith, the answer was inherent to the medium. "The Lego form allows other versions of these characters to be brought together," he told Variety. "It's sort of a universal solvent for the imagination."

"Our Selena Kyle Catwoman can be both dressed as Zoe Kravitz in Matt Reeves' The Batman and later in an outfit which iconically was worn by Michelle Pfeiffer in Batman Returns. In a Lego game, that is the same character — that is Catwoman."

— Jonathan Smith, Strategic Director, TT Games (Variety, May 22, 2026)

That principle extends to every major character. Catwoman shifts between three big-screen interpretations. The Penguin toggles between Colin Farrell's prosthetic-heavy take from The Batman and Danny DeVito's grotesque 1992 version. Even the game's open-world Gotham is designed to hold all these contradictions in check — a city that feels consistent enough to inhabit while housing wildly different Batman mythologies.

Joker's Journey Through the Decades

Perhaps the most ambitious example of this "universal solvent" idea is the game's treatment of the Joker. Rather than picking a single interpretation, TT Games wrote him as a single character whose transformation across the story mirrors his real-world evolution through different films and comics.

Smith walked Variety through the full arc: the Joker begins as a low-level criminal working for Carmine Falcone in the back alleys of Gotham. He then falls into a vat of acid as the Red Hood — a nod to the character's comic origins. When he emerges, white-faced and sharply dressed, it's unmistakably Jack Nicholson's 1989 performance. From there, the game tracks his escalation through increasingly erratic, dangerous behavior that eventually echoes Heath Ledger's portrayal in The Dark Knight.

Worth noting: The Red Hood origin storyline seen in the game has roots in the 1988 Batman comic The Killing Joke, written by Alan Moore — one of the most influential Batman stories ever published.

"They all fit together on the journey that we take these characters over the course of the narrative," Smith told Variety. It's the kind of feat that likely only works in a LEGO game, where the inherent absurdity of the medium gives dramatic permission to do things no straight-faced Batman story ever could.

Every Batman, Every Era — 100 Costumes and Counting

The scope of Batman versions in the game is genuinely staggering. Smith confirmed the complete roster spans the character's entire history: Adam West's 1966 TV hero, every major big-screen Batman from Michael Keaton to Robert Pattinson (including Ben Affleck and George Clooney), Will Arnett's minifigure from The Lego Batman Movie, and newer comics iterations like Absolute Batman and the Victorian-era Gotham by Gaslight.

The game ships with 100 collectible costumes for playable characters — and Smith noted the design team actually created "many more" that didn't make the final cut. To get around Gotham in style, players can choose between multiple Batmobiles: the red-striped 1966 classic, Pattinson's muscle car from The Batman, and a Bat-cycle.

Quick fact: Among the game's more obscure villain inclusions are Condiment King and Kite-Man — deep-cut Batman antagonists that will mean a great deal to longtime comics readers and nothing at all to newcomers, which is exactly the balance TT Games was going for.

The Arkham Inspiration — and Where TT Games Drew the Line

The Rocksteady Arkham Trilogy comparisons started appearing in previews months ago. It's now confirmed that Rocksteady itself served as a co-developer on the project — which made the following quote from design director Jimmy Sedota all the more interesting.

"It's an honour to be honest. Of course, we looked at it for reference. It's a great game. It makes you feel like Batman, right, just very easily mashing a button and you feel like Batman, so we definitely took it as a source of inspiration, but we did build this from the ground up for what we thought was the best LEGO Batman game, and so we made different choices to ensure that it was right for our audience."

— Jimmy Sedota, Design Director, TT Games (GAMINGbible, May 22, 2026)

That distinction matters. Legacy of the Dark Knight has Arkham-style combat DNA, but its design priority was making every character feel meaningfully different — something Sedota described as essential to keeping a long game fresh. "We wanted to provide more depth in our gameplay and more variety in our gameplay," he told GAMINGbible. "We felt like this was the right way to do that, to ensure that things didn't get kind of stale and repetitive several hours into the game."

The result: characters have unique abilities tied to who they are, not just their combat style. Catwoman cracks safes. Commissioner Gordon doesn't. Batman and Robin move through the world in different ways to Nightwing or Talia al Ghul. New Bat-Family members are introduced throughout the campaign specifically to shake up the mechanics and introduce fresh gameplay loops.

Riddler Puzzles: Something for Everyone, a Challenge for Completionists

One of the most discussed elements of Legacy of the Dark Knight post-launch has been its Riddler puzzles, which GAMINGbible's review called "a particular favourite." Sedota confirmed the design philosophy was deliberately tiered.

"We tried to hit a wide spectrum," he said. "Some of them are a little bit simpler, a little more straightforward, but that is an area where we also want some of those puzzles to make you kind of stop and think for a minute. We never wanted them to be too frustrating, obviously, or too challenging or annoying."

The key, Sedota explained, was variety: no two Riddler puzzles use exactly the same solution type, and all of them require different character combinations — which feeds back into the broader design goal of making every member of the Bat-Family feel useful. For completionists specifically, some puzzles are intentionally at the harder end of the difficulty range.

The Numbers: 30,000 Players on Day One

All of those design decisions appear to have paid off. As of the morning of May 23, Legacy of the Dark Knight peaked at over 30,000 concurrent players on Steam alone — figures that don't include PlayStation 5, Xbox Series X|S, or any other platform. The game has accumulated more than 3,000 Steam reviews with a Steambase Player Score of 98/100, sustaining its "Overwhelmingly Positive" rating well past the initial launch rush.

Combined with its Metacritic score of 84 — the highest ever for a LEGO game, beating LEGO Star Wars: The Skywalker Saga's 83 — Legacy of the Dark Knight is putting up numbers that suggest it's reached well beyond the LEGO franchise's existing audience into the wider action-adventure market.

What's Next: The Mayhem Collection

TT Games hasn't announced any additional post-launch content beyond what was already confirmed. The Mayhem Collection DLC — included in the Deluxe Edition and available separately — is due in September 2026. It adds a Joker and Harley Quinn story mission, seven new suits, a new Batmobile, and five Batcave props.

Given the reception so far, it would be surprising if that's the end of the post-launch roadmap — but for now, TT Games hasn't said more. We'll update this page if further DLC is announced.
