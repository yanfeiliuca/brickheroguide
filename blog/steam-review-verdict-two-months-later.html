Two Months Later: What LEGO Batman Legacy's Steam Reviews Actually Say

Published: July 11, 2026 · 6 min read

LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight has been out for almost two months now, and the Steam review pool has grown large enough to show a stable pattern rather than launch-week noise. The game currently holds Steam's "Overwhelmingly Positive" rating across 11,600+ reviews, and games-analytics firm Alinea Analytics ran a dedicated sentiment pass on that review pool shortly after launch, putting the positive score at 96%. Two months on, the same specific things players praise, and the same specific things they complain about, keep showing up. Here's the actual breakdown, not just the headline star rating.

What Reviewers Keep Praising

The consistent throughline in positive reviews is the combat. Alinea's sentiment analysis found players repeatedly describing the dodge, counter, and focus system as a faithful riff on the Rocksteady Arkham trilogy — not a surprise, since Arkham Asylum's combat is still the template most brawlers get measured against. Reviewers also single out the narrative for successfully blending storylines from the Batman movies, animated series, comics, and the Arkham games into one coherent thread that respects the character's decades of media history, a detail that lines up with the game's own marketing pitch of covering "almost 90 years" of Batman lore.

Two smaller but recurring compliments: the quality of non-English dubs, and a soundtrack reviewers describe as memorable rather than functional. Taken together, Alinea's read is that most reviewers treat the production values as AAA-tier, a step up from the lighter production polish usually associated with LEGO games.

The Complaints That Won't Go Away

The negative side of the ledger is smaller but persistent, and it clusters around a few specific, recurring issues rather than being spread thin across many small gripes.

Frame rate drops. Alinea's review sentiment data flags frame rate drops during open-world exploration and split-screen co-op as the most commonly cited technical issue — notably, even on systems that meet the recommended specs. That tracks with what we found benchmarking the PC and Steam Deck versions for our own settings guide: the game's shader compilation pipeline has known gaps that cause stutter no graphics setting fully eliminates.

Save file corruption. This is a separate issue from the frame rate complaints, and it's still showing up in Steam's community discussions well after launch. Players report save files becoming corrupted or deleted outright, forcing them to restart progress; official WB Games support guidance points toward backup save files (named BackupCopy_SaveSlot_0_TT.sav and BackupCopy_GlobalSaveSlot_TT.sav) as a partial workaround, though it doesn't work for everyone. It's worth being precise here: Update 1.006 (June 2) fixed a specific Mr. Freeze boss crash that blocked a story trophy, but that patch did not target the broader save corruption reports, which continue to surface in Steam discussions.

No online co-op. Legacy of the Dark Knight supports couch co-op only, splitting the screen vertically between two local players. There is no online multiplayer option, following the same local-only pattern as TT Games' previous LEGO titles. For a family-oriented game where shared play is a core selling point, reviewers and outlets covering the co-op mode consistently flag this as a real gap rather than a minor omission — friends who don't share a couch simply can't play together.

Why this matters for the DLC: The Mayhem Collection DLC, arriving September 18, 2026 alongside the Switch 2 launch, adds a new story mission and Mayhem Mode built around Joker and Harley Quinn. None of the reporting on the DLC so far mentions online co-op being added, so the local-only limitation looks set to carry over into the expansion.

"It Doesn't Feel Like a LEGO Game" — And That's Deliberate

The most interesting critical thread isn't really a bug complaint at all. Alinea's sentiment analysis found a segment of longtime LEGO game fans flagging the departure from the series' signature mechanics: no red brick cheats, no free-play character requirements, no stud multipliers, and no traditional minikit collection. Legacy of the Dark Knight launched with seven confirmed playable characters (plus three more players discovered were secretly unlockable), a much tighter roster than the 60-100+ characters typical of past mainline LEGO entries. Reviews describe the result as feeling "more Arkham than Lego" — intended as both a compliment and a complaint depending on who's writing it.

That tighter scope is also tied to a price complaint: the $70–$90 price range (depending on edition) is being flagged by the traditional LEGO game audience as steep relative to a smaller character roster, especially compared to genre norms where LEGO games have historically shipped with a much larger cast. It's a fair contrast for players expecting a classic LEGO game rather than an Arkham-style reinvention of one.

The Bigger Picture

None of this changes the headline number. A 96% positive Steam score two months after launch, sitting alongside an "Overwhelmingly Positive" rating across 11,600+ reviews, is a strong result by any standard, and it's a stronger result than most licensed games manage this far past launch. But the specific, repeated nature of the complaints — frame rate drops, save corruption, no online co-op, and the mechanical departure from classic LEGO design — suggests these aren't launch-week teething problems that quietly went away. They're the fixed cost of the game's Arkham-style reinvention, and TT Games appears to have decided that trade-off was worth making. Whether the Mayhem Collection DLC in September addresses any of it, particularly the still-thin character roster, is the next thing worth watching.
