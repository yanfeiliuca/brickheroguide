Two Months Later, Critics Are Still Reviewing LEGO Batman Legacy — WayTooManyGames Gives It 8.5/10

Published: July 18, 2026 · 7 min read

Most games get reviewed in the first week and are forgotten by outlets soon after. LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight is still picking up fresh critic coverage nearly two months after its May 22, 2026 launch. On July 12, gaming outlet WayTooManyGames published a full review — written by Leo Faria after playing on PS5 — and landed on a Final Verdict of 8.5/10, one of the more enthusiastic scores the game has received from any outlet.

The review is worth a closer look not just for the number, but for what it praises: a design philosophy the writer sums up as "quality over quantity," a phrase that cuts against nearly two decades of TT Games' own LEGO formula.

The Score Breakdown

WayTooManyGames scores games across four weighted categories: Graphics (25%), Gameplay (25%), Sound (10%), and Fun Factor (40%). Here's how Legacy of the Dark Knight landed in each:

WayTooManyGames Review Scorecard
Graphics8.0
Gameplay8.5
Sound10
Fun Factor9.0
Final Verdict8.5

The perfect 10 for Sound is notable — the review singles out the voice cast, and specifically calls out Matt Berry's performance as Bane as a highlight worth building a joke around: "If that isn't the pinnacle of perfection, then what is?"

Why "Quality Over Quantity" Is the Whole Pitch

The review's central argument is about scope, not spectacle. Past TT Games LEGO titles — the reviewer specifically references the four-year-old LEGO Star Wars: The Skywalker Saga — built their appeal around sheer volume: dozens of hub worlds, hundreds of unlockable characters, an avalanche of collectibles. Legacy of the Dark Knight inverts that. Players only control seven characters across the campaign, and according to the review, each one has "completely different movesets, attack patterns, secondary weapons, and unlockable outfits" rather than being a reskinned version of the same core moveset.

That tracks with what we've documented elsewhere on this site: the game ships with 21 main missions, four Gotham islands to explore, and a cast built around depth rather than breadth. The review frames the trade-off directly — you lose the completionist thrill of unlocking "300 different Batman heroes and villains," but you gain tighter combat and a more focused story.

Context: This isn't the first outlet to make the Arkham comparison — we broke down how TechRadar, ScreenRant, and Destructoid measured the game against Rocksteady's trilogy in our Arkham comparison roundup. WayTooManyGames goes further, calling it "the closest brand new iteration of an Arkham game we'll get nowadays" and preferring its open world to 2022's Gotham Knights.

A Love Letter Told Through Movie History

The other angle the review highlights is structural: the story isn't just "generic Batman plot," it's staged as a guided tour through decades of live-action Batman film history. According to the review, the game opens with Bruce Wayne training under Ra's al Ghul in the Tibetan mountains — a direct nod to Batman Begins — before the plot moves through beats echoing 1989's Batman, then Batman Returns, then Batman & Robin, reframed with "brand new character interactions" and comedic sideplots layered on top.

It's a structure that only works if the writing lands, and the review is blunt that not every joke does — "a barrage of jokes in which not all of them stick" — but calls the overall voice acting and pacing strong enough to carry it. Combat, meanwhile, is described as a direct descendant of the Arkham series' combo system, inherited wholesale rather than reinvented, but effective regardless.

Why a Second-Wave Review Still Matters

Launch-week scores from IGN (8/10) and GamesRadar (4/5) set the game's initial reputation, and Steam's "Overwhelmingly Positive" tag from over 11,600 reviews has held since launch. What's unusual is how long the critical conversation has kept going. Earlier reviewers like GamingTrend's Joey Caplan, writing back on May 19, made a similar Arkham comparison and landed on a "Great" 80/100 verdict, praising the game's Gotham City design while flagging weaker boss fights and an uneven mid-game narrative — a more mixed take than WayTooManyGames', but arriving at a similar starting premise. Seeing outlets still publishing fresh, full-length reviews seven weeks after launch is a signal of a longer tail of interest than most licensed LEGO titles get, and it sets a fairly high bar heading into the Mayhem Collection DLC on September 18, 2026.

Looking ahead: If critics are still finding fresh angles on the base game two months out, expect another wave of coverage when Joker and Harley Quinn join the roster with the Mayhem Collection DLC — especially given how much of the current critical praise is tied to how distinct each of the existing seven characters feels to play.

Quick Recap

  • WayTooManyGames (July 12, 2026) scored LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight 8.5/10 — Graphics 8.0, Gameplay 8.5, Sound 10, Fun Factor 9.0.
  • The review's core praise is the "quality over quantity" design: 7 characters, each with unique movesets, instead of TT Games' usual huge roster.
  • The story is framed as a guided tour through Batman film history, from a Batman Begins-style origin through Batman (1989), Batman Returns, and Batman & Robin.
  • It's the latest in a string of critic reviews still being published nearly two months post-launch, alongside earlier takes from GamingTrend, IGN, and GamesRadar.
