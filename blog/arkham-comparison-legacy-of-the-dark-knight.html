How LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight Stacks Up Against Batman: Arkham

Published: July 14, 2026 · 6 min read

Nobody reviewing LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight at launch could avoid the comparison. ScreenRant's review ran under the headline "The Arkham Successor We Need AND Deserve." Destructoid titled its verdict "Brick by brick, Arkham is rebuilt." TechRadar put it more bluntly in its subhead: "Arkham, Ar-saw, Ar-conquered?" Three outlets, three separate reviewers, and the same reference point every time — Rocksteady's Batman: Arkham trilogy (Asylum, City, Knight). Two months on from launch, it's worth asking how much of that comparison actually holds up once you look past the headlines.

Combat Built From the Same Blueprint

Destructoid's Andrej Barovic was the most direct about the mechanical overlap, writing that the combat "is a carbon copy of what was in the Arkham games, but also feels very, very familiar to the recent Spider-Man games." His review describes a focus bar that fills through combat and lets you finish enemies immediately or land heavy damage on tougher foes, alongside dedicated counter and dodge inputs — the same three-pillar structure (attack, counter, dodge) that defined Arkham Asylum's free-flow combat back in 2009. TechRadar's Josephine Watson reached a similar conclusion from the player's seat, describing combos that build as heroes "attack, dodge, counter, and land satisfying environmental takedowns," punctuated by comic-book "THAKK!" and "KAPOW!" effects on screen.

The game backs this system with the three difficulty tiers documented in our own difficulty modes guide: Classic and Caped Crusader carry no fail state, while Dark Knight mode removes the safety net and, according to Watson's review, makes enemies noticeably more dangerous — though she also flagged a bug where enemies could see through walls on that highest setting, something she hoped would be patched.

A Familiar Gotham, Rebuilt in Plastic

The Arkham comparison extends past combat into the open world itself. Barovic called Gotham "certainly one of the best open worlds that I've experienced in the last few years," adding that its neon-lit, Art Deco skyline "could just as easily be confused for an Arkham Knight setting rather than a LEGO one." ScreenRant's Austin King made the same point about scale and density: with Riddler Trophies, WayneTech Caches, and Batmobile Trials scattered across the map, "anyone who's played Arkham Knight will especially feel right at home." Our own collectibles guide puts a number on that density — 247+ items spread across four Gotham islands, more than double what most reviewers expected walking in.

Traversal draws the same lineage. Gliding between skyscrapers with a grapple, prowling rooftops in Detective Mode-style scan vision, and free-roaming between story missions are all structurally lifted from the Rocksteady formula, just reskinned with LEGO's brick-built aesthetic and slapstick tone.

Where the Comparison Breaks Down

None of the three reviews treated Legacy of the Dark Knight as a straight clone, and each flagged specific places where it falls short of its inspiration. Watson's biggest complaint was stealth: "the stealth is where the Arkham comparisons fall short; stealth in Legacy of the Dark Knight is passable but unremarkable, and you have fewer tools to inspire fear in a room full of enemies or disappear if you're spotted." She was also unimpressed by the weapon skill tree, calling it "a rather uninspiring array of movesets and upgrades," and noted the game is "surprisingly demanding performance-wise" even on a custom PC and Steam Deck.

The roster is the other major departure. Watson pointed out that the playable cast tops out at seven characters (plus the three community-discovered hidden characters), compared to "over 100 in the most recent Lego Batman game" — a deliberate trade-off that favors Arkham-style depth per character over the traditional LEGO game's sprawling, shallow roster. See our all-characters unlock guide for how each of the seven plays.

The scoring split: ScreenRant awarded a perfect 10/10 (gameplay 10, visuals 10, story 9, audio 9). Destructoid landed at 8.5/10, citing repetitive puzzle design and an uneven story structure. TechRadar didn't publish a numeric score but was the most reserved of the three, closing its verdict with: "it made me want to play Batman: Arkham instead."

The Verdict: A Worthy Successor, Not a Replacement

ScreenRant's King summarized the consensus best: "If there were a Venn diagram of Batman, LEGO, and Arkham fans, Legacy of the Dark Knight would be perfectly at its center." That's the throughline across all three reviews — this isn't Rocksteady's Arkham series with a LEGO skin slapped on, and it isn't a traditional LEGO game with combat borrowed from elsewhere. It's a genuine hybrid, built by TT Games on Unreal Engine 5 specifically to chase that Arkham-style feel within a family-friendly, brick-built frame. Whether that hybrid works for you likely comes down to which half of the equation you value more: players craving true Arkham-grade stealth and combat depth will still notice the gaps, while LEGO fans missing the old cheat-code chaos will notice the tighter, more serious structure. For everyone in between, three independent reviews agree it's the closest thing to a new Arkham game currently on shelves.
