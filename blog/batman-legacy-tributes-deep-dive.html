Every Batman Movie, BTAS & Comics Tribute in LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight

Published: June 18, 2026 · 7 min read

It has been nearly a month since LEGO Batman: Legacy of the Dark Knight launched, and the Batman-mythology Easter eggs hidden throughout the game keep accumulating. While the internet quickly catalogued the pop-culture nods to films like American Psycho and games like Resident Evil 4, an equally rich layer of in-universe tributes to Batman’s own 87-year history has been hiding in plain sight. On June 8, DC.com writer Joshua Lapin-Bertone catalogued his own favorites, confirming a remarkable number of deep-cut nods to the Dark Knight’s film, animation, comics, and gaming legacy. Here is a complete breakdown.

Source note: The Easter eggs in this article are drawn from DC.com’s official feature (June 8, 2026) by Joshua Lapin-Bertone, cross-referenced with gameplay footage. All entries are verified in the released game.

The Kevin Conroy Tribute

The most emotionally resonant Easter egg in the entire game requires no interaction to find — just a walk through the open world. Somewhere in Gotham City, players will come across a billboard advertising Gotham Radio After Hours with Conroy, the voice of Gotham’s night. It is an unmistakable tribute to the late Kevin Conroy, the definitive voice actor behind the animated Batman from 1992 onward.

Conroy voiced Batman in Batman: The Animated Series, Batman Beyond, the Arkham game series, and dozens of animated films over three decades. He passed away in November 2022. TT Games’ decision to immortalize him with a permanent in-game presence rather than a splash screen or loading-screen dedication is, as Lapin-Bertone put it, something “words can’t express.” The billboard is there every playthrough, for every player, in the city that Conroy helped define.

Batman: The Animated Series Tributes

BTAS gets arguably the most elaborate tribute of any single Batman property in the game, and it arrives at a dramatically perfect moment.

Chapter 4 Opening — Confirmed

Shot-for-Shot BTAS Intro Recreation

The opening cutscene of Chapter Four is a shot-for-shot remake of the Batman: The Animated Series intro sequence, with Robin added alongside Batman. Shirley Walker’s legendary score is used, and according to Lapin-Bertone, “every single detail you can imagine is recreated” in LEGO form. This is not a loose homage — it is a precise, loving reconstruction of one of the most iconic animated sequences in superhero history.

PS5 Trophy — Confirmed

“Let’s See What Batgirl Can Do”

On PlayStation 5, unlocking Batgirl earns a trophy titled “Let’s see what Batgirl can do.” This is the exact line Barbara Gordon spoke after suiting up in the BTAS episode “Shadow of the Bat, Part One.” It is the kind of Easter egg that rewards series veterans without ever confusing newcomers.

Batman Film Chapter Tributes

The game’s six-chapter structure maps closely to Batman’s cinematic history, and TT Games has seeded each chapter with props, dialogue, and environmental design that quietly reflect that era.

The Bat-Signal That Changes By Chapter

Pay close attention to Gotham’s sky throughout the campaign. The Bat-Signal changes its design depending on which chapter is active. Chapter Two, for instance, draws heavily from Tim Burton’s Batman films — and the Bat-Signal visible during that chapter matches its design from Batman (1989) and Batman Returns (1992). It is a small detail that rewards attentive players and makes the open world feel genuinely chapter-specific rather than generic.

Chapter 2 — Tim Burton era

The Catwoman Mannequin Scene

When playing as Catwoman in Chapter Two, the game recreates the iconic scene from Batman Returns where Michelle Pfeiffer’s Catwoman uses her whip to knock the heads off department store mannequins. The game lets you do it yourself. This is not a cutscene — it is a playable recreation of one of the most memorable moments in Batman film history.

Chapter 2 — Tim Burton era

Elevator Music at Shreck’s Department Store

While riding the elevator in Shreck’s Department Store — itself a Batman Returns location — an elevator-music arrangement of Danny Elfman’s Batman theme plays in the background. Given the game’s generally bombastic score, this quiet, Muzak-style rendition of the most famous Batman theme ever written is one of the game’s subtler jokes.

Chapter 3 — Batman Forever era

Dick Grayson’s Parents Survive

Chapter Three recreates Two-Face’s circus attack from Batman Forever — but with a twist. This time, Dick Grayson’s parents survive the attack. Bruce Wayne adopts him anyway. As Lapin-Bertone notes, “which is kind of wild,” and it is: the game deliberately changes the tragedy at the heart of Robin’s origin, which raises interesting questions about what kind of Batman this version of Bruce Wayne is.

Chapter 1 — The Flugelheim Museum

Prince’s “Partyman” and the Bat-Grey Ghost

The Flugelheim Museum level (from Tim Burton’s 1989 Batman) features boomboxes throughout the level playing Prince’s “Partyman” — the track the Joker blasted during his museum rampage in the film. Destroy all the boomboxes for a bonus, but the music stops. Additionally, the museum’s iconic Batman comic covers are recreated in LEGO form, except Batman is replaced by the Grey Ghost — a knowing in-universe swap.

Batman Video Game Tributes

Legacy of the Dark Knight does not just celebrate Batman on film and in animation. It also pays tribute to the Dark Knight’s history in interactive entertainment — including the studio’s own legacy work.

Chapter 5 Mission — Confirmed

Batman: Arkham Asylum Opening Recreated

Chapter Five contains a mission that recreates the opening sequence of Batman: Arkham Asylum (2009). Rocksteady’s Arkham series is widely credited with establishing the template for modern Batman games — including the detective vision, the freeflow combat system, and the perched-gargoyle stealth. Having TT Games explicitly recreate Rocksteady’s most famous opening in LEGO form is an act of remarkable generosity toward a competitor, and a clear acknowledgment of what Arkham meant to the character’s gaming legacy.

Unlockable Costume — Confirmed

The 1990 NES Batman Suit

Among the unlockable costumes is the purple 8-bit Batsuit from the 1990 NES Batman game. For players of a certain age, that purple silhouette jumping across the Axis Chemical plant is burned into memory. Its appearance here completes a lineage that spans 36 years of Batman gaming history.

Batman Comics Deep Cuts

The open world is littered with references to Batman’s comics history that go considerably deeper than most players will catch on a first pass.

Open World — Confirmed

Court of Owls Throughout Gotham

Hidden Owl and Talon statues, graffiti referencing the Court, and flyers with cryptic messages are scattered throughout Gotham City. Scott Snyder and Greg Capullo’s Court of Owls — introduced in 2011 — is one of the most critically acclaimed modern Batman stories, and the game treats it as a persistent, worldbuilding-level presence rather than a named Easter egg.

Open World — Confirmed

Loeb & Sale’s Comic Store

While exploring Gotham, players can find Loeb & Sale’s Comic Store — named after Jeph Loeb and Tim Sale, the creative team behind Batman: The Long Halloween (1996–1997), one of the most influential Batman comics ever published. The shop’s display window contains LEGO versions of iconic Batman covers and one of DC’s old logos.

Open World — Confirmed

The Flips at the Iceberg Lounge

A poster for a band called The Flips hangs in the Iceberg Lounge. This is a reference to Showcase #59 (1965), in which the Teen Titans helped a teeny bopper surfer band called The Flips. The comics source material is over 60 years old. This is exactly the kind of “deep cut” that the game’s developers confirmed they intended — a reward exclusively for dedicated DC historians.

Open World — Confirmed

Salvage Maroni

A Gotham City junkyard named Salvage Maroni is a play on Sal Maroni, the mob boss who disfigured Harvey Dent and inadvertently created Two-Face. It is the kind of background world-building that makes Gotham feel like a city with history, rather than a game level with landmarks.

How Many More Are There?

DC.com’s Joshua Lapin-Bertone, writing after completing the main story, noted that he still had “bonuses to unlock, areas of the city to explore and more surprises to find,” and that Legacy of the Dark Knight is “a treasure trove of Easter eggs” where he had “only scratched the surface.” Given the density of verified tributes already catalogued — across film, animation, comics, and gaming — that assessment seems entirely plausible.

What makes these tributes different from the game’s pop-culture Easter eggs is their intentionality. References to Batman Returns or the BTAS intro are not jokes — they are expressions of gratitude. TT Games appears to have built Legacy of the Dark Knight not just as a game, but as a love letter to everyone who has made Batman what he is over the past 87 years. That includes Kevin Conroy, whose voice in the Gotham night will apparently now ring out forever from a billboard above the city he spent his career defending.
